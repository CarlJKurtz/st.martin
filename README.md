# St. Martin
The typeface St. Martin highlights a typographic oddity found in the works of the 19th-century Parisian printer Jean-Alexis Rouchon and mixes it with the supposed neutrality of a sans-serif base.

## About
St. Martin was created in 2024 by Carl J. Kurtz for the class Ghostly Presences at the HAW-Hamburg, in which the students examined the works of the 19th-century Parisian printer Jean-Alexis Rouchon.

![](https://github.com/carljkurtz/st.martin/blob/main/Readme-Images/readme_image-1.png?raw=true)[^*]

Some lowercase letters in some of his works (Most notably a, r, and n.) show an almost pixel-like bracket which looks like parts of the letters were shifted horizontally. This shift is always the height of slab serifs and occurs every time a curved shape meets a stem. Because of the consistency, I am sure, that this must be a stylistic decision and not a mistake. We did speculate on the origins of this effect. Maybe the typeface was constructed from different overlapping components and applied by a stenciling process. But then, where would you reuse the bowl of the lowercase a?

![](https://github.com/carljkurtz/st.martin/blob/main/Readme-Images/readme_image-2.png?raw=true)[^*]

![](https://github.com/carljkurtz/st.martin/blob/main/Readme-Images/readme_image-3.png?raw=true)[^*]

![](https://github.com/carljkurtz/st.martin/blob/main/Readme-Images/readme_image-4.png?raw=true)[^*]

To highlight these typographical peculiarities we chose to apply them onto the sans-serif base Amiamie, an open-source typeface created by Mirat-Masson in 2022. Amiamie itself is based on an open-source typeface called Aileron, designed by Sora Sagano in 2014 for TipoType.

This Font was designed for the 2024 class Ghostly Presences at the HAW in Hamburg under the guidance of Pierre Pané-Farré and Simon Thiefes.
